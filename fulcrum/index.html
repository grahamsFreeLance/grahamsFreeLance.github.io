About Us:

Fulcrum is a new boatbuilding startup run by David Clark, a twelve year veteran of the International Canoe fleet. He has apprenticed for ten years under his father Steve Clark, the former owner of Vanguard Sailboats and a veteran designer and builder. Steve taught Dave everything he knows. Fulcrum's mission is to join Steve's knowledge of peak performance IC design with his knowledge of cost-effective manufacturing to create affordable world-class ICs.

Why an International Canoe?


The last thing the sailing world needs is to be splintered by yet another one-design class. The International Canoe class is a uniquely fast, iconic and simple boat. The sliding seat technology allows for unparalleled low beam and high righting moment, for a dinghy, with less drag than the traditional wings and trapezes system. The IC is a development class, which enables a home builder to work within relatively forgiving tolerances and to experiment with a broad number of fun ideas. Machete, designed in the fall of 2014 on data from the 2014 World Championship is the newest design and sports the most aggressive lines of the currently dominant “fine bow, fanned stern” design school. Her bow measuring sixteen degrees in overhead view is the sharpest of any IC in the world, making for excellent wave-piercing. Further, the canoe class is a welcoming and skilled group of sailors with regular racing in numerous areas and a world championship every three years. Few other kit boats come with such a luxury. With more than a century of history and development and an international challenge cup beaten only by the America’s Cup for longest standing perpetual sailing trophy in contest, just owning an IC is something to be proud of.

So how hard is it to build?


It isn’t. Designer Steve Clark pulled out all the stops on ease of build when he designed Machete. The objective was to make a kit so easy that it could be put together by a college freshman in his or her dorm room. Fulcrum’s Dave Clark deliberately separated himself from the bulk of his tools and rented an 800 square foot storage space in upstate New York to build it in, thus simulating a more frugal home-building environment. The first Machete was built with a basic bag of tools. The two most heavily used weapons were a cabinet-makers file and a caulk-gun loaded with West System Six10. Since the Machete hull in complete form only weighs ~60 pounds, and the hull is only 30 inches wide, picking it up to stow or flip throughout the build is as easy as picking it up and stowing or flipping it. Further, since the hull is so skinny, the boat can be walked out of a standard sized door while being held by one man at its middle. The shape and weight make for an almost comically easy boatbuilding experience.

How much does it really take to build it?


The first machete build took about 40 full days to complete, which roughly equates to an off-season’s worth of weekends. Total project costs, including the kit, building the dolly and assuming one can find secondhand sails, should be around $7000 dollars. However, if just getting to the water is the objective, far cheaper avenues can be found by way of secondhand aluminum spars, or taking parts wholesale from a penultimate IC.
